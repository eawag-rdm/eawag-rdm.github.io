Why Open Publciation Data?

  1. Required by publisher / funding agency
  2. More citations
  3. More potential collaborators
  4. Better visibility
  5. Good scientific practice (reproducibility)
  6. Ethical obligation

How to prepare Open Publciation Data?

Rules of Thumb:
  • Can a researcher build on this dataset without having to contact me?
  • standardized and widely spread?
  • implemented on several major operating systems?
  • as simple as possible?
  • machine readable?
  • common in my field?
  • not tainted by patents?
  • well documented
  • complete
  • "good" file formats

A common structure:

  1. raw data (e.g. straight from the logger, minimal cleaning/sorting)
  2. pre-processed data
  3. data directly underlying the figures and tables of the publciation
  4. code for pre-processing
  5. analysis & plotting code
  6. documentation (e.g. a "README.txt", the manuscript)

Licenses & Legalese

1. Code

  • Is subject to copyright.
  • Make sure you apply a license to it, else nobody can fully use it in a scientific context.
  • Make sure it is an OSI - approved license.

Use the MIT License. If you want to use one of the GNU Public Licenses instead, you already know that.

Licenses & Legalese

2. Text

  • Is subject to copyright.
  • If this is the published paper you might have given away the copyright and are not allowed to distribute your own work!
  • Often you are allwed to distribute a pre-print or a post-print instead.
  • Check the Sherpa/Romeo list (and the relevant links there pointing to the individual publishers) to figure out what you exactly you are allowed to distribute.
  • Make sure that essential text to describe the data (usually the "Methods" section) is present in any case. You can always cut & paste the "Methods" part into a text-file (US: "fair use", CH: URG Art. 25).

Licenses & Legalese

3. Data you produced

  • Is generally not subject to copyright (at least in CH and DE).
  • Actual law is a palimpset of international treaties, changing national laws mostly meant for music tapes, and hazy definitions -- a mess --.
  • Realistically you can not do anything to legally prevent other people from using your data in whatever way they like. Except: Just keep it secret!

To avoid superflous questions and encourage people to give you proper credit for your data:

  • Put the Creative Commons "CC BY" on your data.
  • Prominently provide a snippet of text that can be copied and pasted to easily reference your data.

Licenses & Legalese

4. Data someone else produced

Sometimes you should include data produced by pother people to ensure completeness and reproducibility.

  • Is generally not subject to copyright (at least in CH and DE).
  • EU countries protect the "intellectual creation of selection and arrangement of databases". Lawyers being lawyers that could be the intellectual creation of putting the column with precip next to the column with intercept.
  • To be on the safe side: re-arrange such data and use only a sub-set if possible.
  • Get permission if this is about a substantial part of a real database.
  • If in doubt: Ask for permission!
  • Give proper credit!
  • If this involves patents, ask a lawyer.

Where to publish?

Avoid publisher-run repositories!

  • not reliable
  • limited findability
  • usually no straight forward API

Prefer community-run and well established respositories

  1. Field specific repositories,
    e.g. Dryad (life-sciences), Pangaea (Earth & Env. Science)
  2. Best general purpose repository: Zenodo

What about the
Eawag
Research
Data
Platform?

  • The ERDP will hold a data package for each Eawag publication by default.
  • Its metadata is a superset of that usually required by external repositories.
  • The expected quality & format is identical to that expected by external repositories.
  • The ERDP will provide a workflow that (semi-)automatically handles submissions to Zenodo (and possibly other repos).
  • Best strategy for your, now: Get in touch with me, submit a data-package to the ERDP, and let me help with the submission to Zenodo.
